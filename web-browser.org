#+title: web-browser

I've started to try to move off of Web Browsers for the most part.
Somewhat ironically this idea got a boost from reading timbl's "This
is for Everyone" and specifically the chapter about the HTML5/WHATWG
schism.

It seems like the vision of the Web as outlined in that book would best
benefit from it being as open as possible, and Web browsers may
incidentally introduce siloing...and modern browsers may more
intentionally introduce moats around those siloes.
While this is certainly preferably to the even narrower and often
darker alternatives of apps, it seems ideal that there should be some
complement to "Anyone can say Anything about Any topic" on the
consumption side, and the necessity of current browsers imposes a
/heavy/ tax on that goal.

This is an area where agents could become useful at least as a
migratory step - though this would still likely be another
manifestation of the [[id:F974B544-7B68-42E0-8978-DF4D1DECACA1][browser-sledgehammer]].
* Web Browsers and Engines

Much of the argument above swirls around the idea of the complexity of
the browser engine and the assertion that if the costs of engine
development are staggering, then the content is behind some
combination of a moat or bloat (which is typically hard to justify).
This includes both rendering engines, and Javascript/ES engines.

While there's a slew of different "browsers" - they tend to use one of
a small number of engines, and those engines that fall from grace at
the tech giants also seem to smoothly descend towards obsolesence.

Some lightweight (and text) browsers use alternative engines, but
much of the modern Web will probably not be supported (where that
level of graceful degradation seems to be a thing of the past).

Something like NetSurf with an optional QuickJS engine would shed the
bloat concerns, and then could be supplemented with behavior to fill
the moat.
