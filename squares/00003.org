#+title: Concept-First Programming Language

I think many years ago I read a section in Programming Language
Pragmatics (a book I don't currently have access to to confirm) to the
effect that programming is a conversation between two people (the
language designer and user) about what they want a computer to
do. I've since also had thoughts about some of the prospective issues
with programming languages and the amount of churn introduced by too
much attention being paid to how things are expressed rather than what
is being done, and for a while nursed an inchoate assertion that
programming languages have been a source of stagnation and bloat in
the industry. Though I have personally waded through a fair amount of
resultant waste (e.g., teams spending months working on a language X
wrapper for a tool written in language Y since they prefer language X
when the wrapper provides no evident benefit over calling the language
Y tool directly), I also appreciate the nuances of the problem.

When thinking about it from a (novice) language design perspective, it
feels as though a major contributing factor is the prevalance of the
languages themselves and the resulting implications.

#+begin_quote
The limits of my language means the limits of my world.
-- [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein][Ludwig Wittgenstein]]
#+end_quote

In terms of computational thinking, software is somewhat unbounded.

#+begin_quote
The programmer, like the poet, works only slightly removed from pure
thought-stuff. He builds his castles in the air, from air, creating by
exertion of the imagination. Few media of creation are so flexible, so
easy to polish and rework, so readily capable of realizing grand
conceptual structures. (This very tractability has its own problems.)
-- Fred Brooks
#+end_quote

While language is a necessary evil of being able to communicate ideas,
it would seem as though constraining it to that communication may help
hedge those limitations that it may impose, and yet for most languages
this does not seem to be the case.

Drawing on the earlier description of programming being a
conversation, there are at least two channels that are immediately
subjected to the limitations of language - how can a programmer convey
what they want to be done, and how can the computer translate that
into something executable. The former is potentially more flexible and
lends itself to different languages that provide different approaches
and paradigms based on needs and tastes. The latter is likely the
hairier one to work through given that it is where the "thought-stuff"
gets broken down into the specifics of the machine that is
available. There can theoretically be a pristine space in the middle
in which the program itself is represented as more abstract concepts.

More often languages seem to follow a more direct path where the input
language is transformed into some form of machine friendly
language. While many languages make use of intermediate forms and
mechanisms such as virtual machines, the output still tends to be
biased towards specific expectations of how it will be executed rather
than what is conceptually desired. This is perfectly reasonable from a
historical perspective both in terms of resource limitations and as an
evolutionary path (and also accounting for lower-level systems
programming where there is a desire to stay closer to the metal).

Like most things, I'd imagine this overlaps with already existing
efforts, but I'm going to work on implementing such a concept-first
language. The language is tentatively called "i.e.", mostly since I
wanted a short prefix, IE seems potentially up for grabs now that
Internet Explorer is dead, and I like the retronym of "in
essence". Initially this is likely to be very unininteresting and just
resemble any of the countless examples of modeling a language in
another language, but I'm also planning on pursuing compilation using
the resulting model.

The core focus to start will be on concept definition itself - details
such as the language that is used and how the concepts are evaluated
and ultimately compiled will be incrementally built on from there.
