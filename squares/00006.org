#+TITLE: AI and The Carousel of Progress

Last week my family and I went to Walt Disney World. Typically
vacations are a time of reflection for me (with quotidian concerns
removed) and this was no exception. One attraction that particularly
resonated with some of my current thoughts was The Carousel of
Progress (which I've probably seen a handful of times - twice in the
past week). I've spent quite a bit of time lately contemplating the
relative return on investment for many of our current technologies,
and the historical perpsectives of the ride along with Disney's
optimistic but dated message certainly fed some of those thoughts.

One quick aside is that from what I saw I appreciate that Disney World
is chock full of "cast members" that could presumably be automated
away, and hope that that is an intentional choice to preserve the feel
and offer employment rather than more of a cold, calculated decision
driven by net costs that can arise from premature adoption of
automation.

The current adoption of AI raises a couple related questions. One
which seems to have an obvious but disappointing answer is how much of
the current wave of automation will be rationalized. Much earlier
automation was justified by (understandably) arguing that it was
taking over work which was some combination of monotonous, dangerous,
and strenuous. What reason will be provided when the jobs being
supplanted fall outside of those criteria and are potentially those
activities for which more time would have been granted by prior
automation? In many/most cases the specific work that AI is doing may
not be particularly "rewarding", but they may be stepping stones that
bolster skills that underpin some of the more sophisticated tasks, or
they may represent work which allow people to keep the lights on while
honing their craft.

"The complementary question is what pursuits we're ostensibly freeing
ourselves up for. While the previous question is unsurprisingly not
touched on on Carousel, this one has a couple nods in the current
incarnation where there's mention of things like having more time to
walk our dogs, along with a discussion of not wanting to have to deal
with things like cooking. Cooking feels like a good example of
something that some people enjoy doing and there's already an
established means to leverage such people to not have to do it
yourself. I think the /AI Slop/ episode of Last Week Tonight also
featured some exectutive mentioning tasks like making music as
something AI will spare us from. The last seen of Carousel features a
couple of the animatronics playing VR video games...is the desired end
state a life of pure leisure? Prior to the trip but appropriate since
it's also Disney my perspective is that we may be on the path to
the life of the humans on WALL-E - being led not to a world of
enrichment but one in which we feed some type of larger machine
through consumption with presumably some ulterior Solyent Greenesque
reason for us to continue to exist.

My main related takeaway from the week is ultimately one of optimisim
driven by freedom which reflects a confluence of these thoughts along
with others such as preferring Knowledge Over Products. While Carousel
certainly didn't provide a lot of deep insight to unravel, it
helped reinforce ideas around having a more critical eye towards
"progress" (and this prompted me to read /A Short History of Progress/).
