#+title: GenAI and Software Engineering

Given the current landscape, it seems worth providing assessments of
[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generative_AI][generative AI]] since much of the content presented herein may seem to
be anachronistic given the spread of such tools.  Professionally I
started using GenAI in earnest in 2025 (primarily [[https://github.com/features/copilot/agents][agentic Copilot]]) and
it has been fairly transformative for my day-to-day work. I tend to
have a few balls in the air at any given time and the use of [[https://github.com/features/copilot][Copilot]]
allows me to delegate that work to a combination of agents and other
programmers. Much of the more toilsome work is effectively pushed to
the background for me, allowing me to request and review such work as
time allows (between meetings, etc.).

There's a fair amount of chatter around the implications of GenAI on
the field of software engineering as a whole. A significant thread which
will underlie some of the other content on this site is that the field
of software engineering has arguably suffered from significant
inefficiency. Through that lens, GenAI can be seen to cut some of the
associated fat. This effectively translates to the reality that knowing
a programming language no longer translates to hireability. From my
perspective (and many others) such skills are a small part of software
engineering, but based on personal experience there are a fair amount of
"coders" out there that seem to rely on their ability to wield
specific tools rather than more essential skills.

The above sentiment primarily focuses on AI absorbing the onerous taks
of code production, but a more extreme model is where an entire
solution is produced through vibe coding. While there is nothing to
prevent vibe coding from being used very widely, it certainly seems
unsafe to blindly rely upon it to produce anything of import. I'm
certainly not about to suggest that the purported risks and hazards
will consistently limit its use, but there should hopefully remain
plenty of cases in which there is clear value and desire to have
solutions that are more intentionally engineered. Similar to the code
production perspective, many (most?) cases likely do not warrant such
attention and may have a lot of bloat that is an artifact of a surge
in reliance on computers without commensurate ease of use - and GenAI
is a possible means to bridge that divide.

What I see as some of the more fundamental aspects of software
engineering that seem less likely to be disrupted in the near term are
largely those that surround disciplines such as [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Requirements_engineering][requirements
engineering]]. The actual production of software itself can be seen as
somewhat secondary to activities such as working out precise
behaviors, having robust approaches for verification and validation,
and optimizing various qualities based on salient tradeoffs. Many of
these seem likely to not only remain valid, but become increasingly
important in a world where GenAI accelerates delivery, and therefore
how far a system may move in a given direction and the consequences of
such motion. While GenAI /can/ help reduce the costs of many types of
change, some changes tend to be more difficult (e.g., those that
impact users), and the provided acceleration may yield amplification
of waste along with the need to work through such changes.

In the mid-term it therefore seems wise to have software
engineers/architects in the mix that are able to keep abreast of many
of the core considerations for the solutions being developed -
particularly given that it is likely to be far messier and higher risk
to approach such issues through reverse engineering. In the longer
term this may shift as AI becomes more trustworthy in being able to
watch itself - in the current LLM-driven world AI can superficially
help with certain aspects, but while it can provide useful
explanations - making contextualized decisions is something that is
likely best left to post-LLM AIs.

GenAI seems to primarily be disturbing those more prescriptive aspects
that seem to be consistently identified as relatively minor, but suck
up too much attention. Concerns such as domain knowledge and modeling
are anecdotally identified as those that are most in need of deeper
attention, but we regularly instead devote our focus on technical
details. The role of "software engineer" could certainly be replaced
by new or existing alternatives such as "solution engineer" or
"requirements engineer", but bringing engineering discipline into the
development of solutions is something that is likely to remain
necessary and (in my experience) tends to be underserved. That role
seems likely to stick around for a bit, though it will likely require
less heads and should be (thankfully) less siloed.
