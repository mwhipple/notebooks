#+TITLE: Information Firehose and Mindful Consumption

One product that is frequently featured when my family and I watch
streaming services is Rocket Money, and we have a running joke around
one fo the commercials where one of the actors says "Rocket Money" in
an odd stoccato way with abnormal emphasis that is seemingly also
supposed to be recall in action but just reads like she's surprised by
what she's saying. When we first saw the commercials, I was thinking I
needed to audit my subscriptions, but could easily just do it myself -
but after prolonged exposure I started to ponder the idea that such
needs are widespread enough to warrant such commercials. While I'd
previously viewed some of those issues as something specific to
myself, this implied that they were more endemic to the modern world.

The main message of Rocket Money is to rein in subscriptions, which
seems like a counteraction to how readily the combination of
subscriptions and automatic payments can lead to unrecognized
waste. This is symptomatic of a wider issue, however, in which various
forms of consumption are made easier and expanded to the point where
we become inattentive or numb to what we are actually consuming. This
is most clearly combatted with minimalism (which I'm inclined
towards), but more basically it can be addressed through something
more akin to mindful consumption - and here consumption is not simply
goods and services but information. This does not extend to conscious
consumption (of which I'm also a proponent) but can remain instead
remain fixed on personal benefit rather than wider
implications. Without such care there are plenty of costs for both the
consumer themselves (monetary, time, in some cases mental health) and
the world (resources consumed and waste produced), and the benefit for
anyone other than the vendors seems nebulous given that without
attention the consumer themselves cannot appreciate what is being
consumed and instead seem likely to just fall into a mindless
equivalent of doom scrolling through existence.

An adopted resolution to the above issue is to simply pay attention to
what is being consumed. This can be accomplished by the strategic
introduction of some minor friction. Things like subscriptions are
readily addressed by actually tracking finances. Things like physical
goods can be addressed by keeping some semblance of inventory. The
most precious commodity of time can be addressed similarly, by just
having taking stock of where it goes and the derived value.

My suggested antidote is the mentioned strategic friction; introducing
appropriate tasks that enjoin us to pay more attention.  The firehose
of the modern world can lock us all into inertial system 1 thinking,
whereas appropriately injected disruption allows us to more
toughtfully anlayze our actions. That such attention seems lacking is
caused by the removal of such friction - there is increasing
automation and ease for the range of associated concerns such as
finances, acquisition and disposal of goods, and information
consumption. The optimal balance is likely to be different for
different people, but there certainly tend to be risks in entering
cycles and piling on; vendors will happily suggest that the solution
is yet another straw to add to your camel, but I'm skeptical about
going deeper into the desert.
