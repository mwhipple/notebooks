#TITLE: Models Are Hypotheses

#+begin_src quote
  All models are wrong, but some are useful
  -- George E. P. Box
#+end_src

A trap that I've encountered a few times when working through refining
models with teams is that there is the expressed desire for a model to
be "right". This typically implies that there is a desire for the
model to attempt to reflect the real world in some way or other
and as often as not introduces noise into the resulting solution
(capturing those details that are /there/ but are not useful). I
believe there's a good video from Eric Evans discussing the Mercator
Projection which helps to reinforce the perspective that models should
be fit for purpose.

Models are inherently simplifications and continuing from the quote
above and more formally analyzed by Godel and others they cannot be
complete and consistent and therefore to some extent are
"wrong". Through the prism of the scientific method, models represent
hypotheses: the hypothesis that a given model delivers relatively
optimal utlitity. This can then draw on other related language to
provide a framework for empirical refinement and assessment of both
models and the resulting solutions.

* On the Outside

Any given solution is expected to have desired effects on its
environment. As touched on above, the derived model therefore
reflects a hypothesis that those aspects that are significant to
producing such effects are workably captured. The process of checking
whether the model is providing the desired value is /validation/. How
well it is doing so can be measured as /effectiveness/.

The above should align with business strategy: those desired effects
should map neatly onto value proposition and if following a
disciplined approach to requirements engineering which stays clear of
the four dark corners, this should be captured as user requirements or
goals.

* On the Inside

The model defines the system's view of the world and it is therefore
designed to act upon that model in defined ways. The process of making
sure that the system is operating as desired in relation to the model
is /verification/. How well it is doing so can be measured as
/efficacy/.

These aspects are more tactical in nature. If following disciplined
requirements engineering these should be captured as system
requirements or specifications (and ideally also left largely to the
owning teams).

* Use of the Above

The above can help inform a framework for how to approach modeling and
solution design as a whole. Beyond the aforementioned bottom-up
problems of pursuing some kind of "correct" model, there can also be
more pervasive issues resulting from a lack of delineation between
these layers and therefore not having a means to assess the model
separately from the implementation. Refining an implementation with an
inefficient/useless model will leave users underserved, and conversely
thinking of the implementation as the model is likely to introduce
churn and the perspective that the strategy is commensurately unstable
and seemingly unsound. Much of this is likely touched on in
requirements engineering literature.
