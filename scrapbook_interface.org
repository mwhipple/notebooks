#+title: scrapbook-interface

* Existing Interfaces

There are a few interface models that currently dominate how we
interact with computer software.

The more primitive option is the command line. These offer
REPL-style (read-eval-print-loop) interactions, and when combined with
concepts such as pipes allow for powerful composition of behavior. One
potential gap, however, is that they are heavily biased towards the
current interaction: while most shells provide facilities such as
history they are an extension to rather than a core part of the way in
which the interface is used.

The more modern alternative is the desktop metaphor. While the goal
itself seems admirable, the realization of providing affordances
analogous to a desktop have seemed elusive - the difference between
the metaphor and reality were identified ~30 years ago by Brooks in
"No Silver Bullet" (and likely others), but seem to anecdotally
reinforce his perception at the time that there's really only one
active item on the desktop at a given time. Compared to a shell, the
desktop is less readily malleable - and the ability for multiple
desktop items to interact remain limited let alone the prospect of
anything like composing something novel.

An additional model to call out are individual processes that provide
a range of prospective behaviors, such as Web browsers and IDEs. While
Web browsers are likely positioned to become a unifying interface,
most of these are likely to just fall victim into the
generalization/specialization - jack of all trades, master of none
dichotomy. It seems far more tenable to think we should have some form
of /space/ in which tools can operate and ideally interoperate, rather
than pursuing a tool to rule them all.

* Forced Integration

Much of the history of software has been afflicted with forced
integration. Tool A speaks plain text, Tool B speaks JSON: how do they
work together? Tool A is in Python, Tool B is in JavaScript, how can
they share logic? This introduces a bunch of toil on the micro level
and expands on the macro level to latent value such as what Thomas
Friedman describes as the "worfklow revolution".

This cost of integration is related to the precision and limitations
of the tools used. If the integration has some rough
edges then things could break...or potentially much worse: do the
wrong thing.

/I/ may not care about those differences. I can certainly
interpret through some of those differences. I may not want to parse
less friendly formats, but I could (though I certainly don't have an
interest in Matrix-style reading binary). More significantly, the
overhead of adapting can drop dramatically: making such data easily
readable avoids the very long tail of having it conform to a precise
set of expectations that has been historically necessary for such
integrations. Overall the person-in-the-loop flow reduces the baseline
costs and then allows for incremental refinements. In this model I
therefore just want somewhere where all of that information can be
collected and then I can process it and refine the tools as warranted.

Now (of course) we can just re-read that last paragraph and replace
any mention of "I" and "person" with "An AI agent" (except the agent
can be expected to go Neo).

* Invasion of Natural Language

One of the most consequential tools to gain popularity in the past few
years is generative AI (surprise!). Beyond the other impacts, it
carries a different interaction model. It is often treated as a
larger-grained component rather than having a more focused purpose
(and agents may be expected to work out and utilize those more focused
tasks themselves).

More importantly, the primary means to interact with an LLM is through
natural language: specialized integrations are therefore
largely unnecessary. While there may be a lot of value in automating
certain asepcts such as context management, the interactions
themselves are relatively unconstrained.

This overall results in a model which is largely in the opposite end
of the spectrum from forced integration. We end up in a state where
we're making heavy use of a tool that doesn't have much integration
cost, and one that as previously mentioned can provide some adaptation
for some of the other integrations.

* Engineering with AI

The use of AI in engineering can be viewed as working at a higher
level of abstraction, and ultimately once dust settles that may be
what is left. Disciplined use of AI does not differ from other
engineering practices in that it should draw on an empirical
methodology. Data should be collected on both the system that is
produced and how the process is used, so that both can be verified and
improved upon as justified.

This leads to the assertion that such information should be surfaced
and tracked somewhere, and it may effectively become the new interface
for the use of AI. If you are developing systems where prompts produce
your code, then that code is no longer your source, your prompts are -
and they should be treated accordingly.

This has clear implications on how we think about aspects such as
maintainability which seem to have plenty of debate in the community,
but it also enjoins us to revisit ideas around how necessary information
is conveyed as part of or colocated with code. While more robust
perspectives such as Bertrand Meyer's Single Model Principle can
likely just be raised a level, without appropriate adaptation there's
likely to be a lot of people that learn the hard way that things like
code are not made more fungible by reducing the cost of production,
but of reproduction.

* Scrapbook Interface

All of the above has led me to adopt what I'll call the Scrapbook
Interface. This is a collection of live notebooks (Jupyter is likely
the most commonly known option, but I'm using Babel in org mode). But
rather than a "notebook" the model is more akin to a scrapbook where
different data can just pop in and out of the different books without
any cohesion or need for something analogous to transcribing.

This draws on a range of existing practices and patterns such as
laboratory notebooks, literate programming, and text user interfaces
such as used in Oberon and Acme. The end result is that I have
repositories of such scrapbooks which I compose and navigate in order
to have those books either invoke functionality or produce artifacts,
and any results and supporting information can be tracked in place.

This allows me to quickly cobble together different tools without
worrying up front about how they will integrate, which can span
packaging, languages, and formats. This includes AI, where different
models and agents could be called or constructed...for the time being
I'm primarily using LangChain and Ollama which can just be dropped
into Python blocks to wire up agents.

The scrapbooks themselves allow me to readily track and navigate
work and collect references. I also use Org Roam which allows the
assorted information to be collected as a graph over time which allows
for associations to evolve more independently of a fixed structure.
This approach as a whole provides me a more intentional way to use all
tools including the new breed of those supporting agentic coding.

Perhaps most significantly, this also lends itself to being
self-reinforcing due to there being a consistent model for context
collection. From an AI-centric perspective this can be repositioned
from the concept of executable /scrapbooks/, to executable
/context/. This can therefore evolve to the equivalent of an
~AGENTS.md~ file or skills files that readily enables both agentic and
personal interactions. Myself and any agents can collaborate on a
shared set of context to produce artifacts, and it can serve as a
readily usable interface for both of us.
