#+title: formal-verification

I have a general interest in formal verification, and I see it as a
potential major component in the evolution of software engineering and
AI. It is nicely complementary in that AI can absorb much of the
tedium of formal verification, and the verification can provide
confidence that the AI is doing what is desired. There's likely some
prism through which Curry-Howard Correspondence can pass to bring us
to some promised land...but I don't have the background to make that
happen.

Here I'm going to collect a quick take on some formal verification
tools that I've used along with a corresponding roadmap to guide
selection. As this covers limited personal experience, there are
plenty of omissions including some major ones.

* idris

Idris allows for constructing provable implementations through
dependent typing. It is also a great language overall. Something like
this _could_ be particularly relevant for the AI use above, and
something that I use personally - but without both a path to
streamline integration with AI and a route to optimize most of the
code without forfeiting some of the safety, it seems likely to be less
tenable for professional use.

* rocq

One major win for this tool in general is the provided
documentation. What seems to be the core differentiator for rocq is
the ability to apply tactics. This would be my choice for doing
something like building out a formal system: composing the assorted
theorems and lemmas to provide guaranteed qualities of constructs.

* [[id:12BC5AB3-D75B-43BC-A51E-C8CD80AAE829][tla+]]

This provides some model checking of a specification, particularly
good for working out prospective issues with distributed and
concurrent systems. This would be/is my primary choice if I were
looking to prove out something like safety/liveness properties of a
design.
